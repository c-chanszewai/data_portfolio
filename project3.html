Fictional Bookstore Inventory, Sales Records, And Customer Survey Analysis
(MySQL + Power BI)

In this project, I practiced web scraping, created two mock datasets, and worked on cleaning up and manipulating data with Excel and Power Query. I also used Power BI’s analytical functions and Excel’s pivot tables to make some visualizations that tell a meaningful story.

Here’s the setup: there’s a small fictional bookstore with 999 titles. I web scraped the fictional book data from books.toscrape.com using Octoparse. For the first part of the project, I put together an inventory spreadsheet and a simple August sales record. With these, I updated stock quantities and carried out some sales analysis.

In the second part, I created a customer survey with 250 responses using Mockaroo (a random data generator). I used the custom list feature on the site to build weighted or dynamic distributions based on data from a 2022 Canadian book consumer survey. The survey results could suggest some marketing strategies the bookstore might consider.

Part 1a - Data cleaning in Excel

I started with two tables from web scraping: an inventory table with title, price, and stock quantity, and a category table with each title’s matching category. The scraped data in the inventory table needed some cleanup, so I did the following:

  1. Used “Flash Fill” and “Find & Replace” to standardize the formatting
  2. Splitted data into separate columns with “Split Text to Columns”
  3. Checked for duplicates using “Conditioning Formatting” and “Countif”
  4. Removed unwanted characters and converted the column from text to numbers with “Substitute” and “Value”
  5. Re-categorized some titles with “Sort”
  6. Combined the two tables (Inventory and Category) with “Xlookup”

The August sales table was put together by selecting books (based on the popular genres obtained from Seattle Library Checkout Records) using “Conditional Statements”.

Part 2b - Customer Survey Analysis

1. Who are our customers?
Most of our customers are middle-aged females, followed closely by women aged 60 and up. The largest group has a Bachelor’s degree, making up about 50%.

2. Are ebook sellers our biggest competitors?
In general, 29% of customers prefer ebooks while 44% still lean towards print books. Interestingly, among customers aged 34 and younger—who have either a high school diploma, an associate’s degree, or a graduate degree—a whopping 59% prefer ebooks! While this group isn’t our main customer base, it’s clear that ebooks are gaining traction with the younger generation. This signals a good opportunity for us to adapt our strategies while ensuring that our primary customers continue enjoying the experience of flipping through physical books. Some ideas could include selling ebook reader accessories and merchandise, and enhancing the in-store experience for the digital generation by providing free Wi-Fi and charging stations.

3. Do our customers visit libraries or bookstores more often?
It’s clear that our customers frequent libraries more often. We could consider partnering with libraries to organize events, boosting their patron attendance while giving readers who don’t want to wait for popular titles a chance to purchase them from us.

4. How can we enhance our customers' browsing and shopping experience to boost sales?
I’ve set up three slicers for the following three tables to help us focus our strategies on specific customer groups. Promotional sales are particularly valued, especially among middle-aged females (our largest patron group). Book clubs are also highly welcomed, particularly by younger customers, and can be a great way to build relationships with them. We can also redesign our genre signage to make it more visually appealing and break down general genres into sub-genres. Since our staff recommendations are appreciated, we can highlight staff picks in some of our newly featured sub-genres. Additionally, the themes of our book clubs and special offers can be centred around popular genres, which are shown in the last chart.

5. What genres are most popular among our customers?
The results can give us valuable insights into which genres we might want to expand in our stock selection and how we allocate shelf space. It’s fascinating to note that popular genres vary across different age groups, so we might consider rotating our featured genres from time to time.
